About

Intro video goes here A 60–90s clip answering "tell me about yourself" & "why security?"

I'm an IT generalist with a security specialty. I got into this field because I like the version of the job where things don't break, whether that's a network, a server, or a project timeline.

Over the past five years I've moved through help desk, systems administration, and network engineering before specializing in detection engineering, vulnerability management, and security automation. That path means I still think in infrastructure terms even when I'm deep in a SIEM, and I run projects the same way: scoped, scheduled, and tracked. I currently run an active home lab covering detection pipelines, MDM, and SIEM integrations to stay sharp on current tooling.

I hold an active CISSP and a Master's in Cybersecurity Management from Purdue (3.82 GPA), I'm a year out from sitting both the PMP and the CISM, and I've placed in national cyber defense competitions including Hivestorm and CCDC.

Why security, specifically? Because I started in help desk, where you only ever see the aftermath of something going wrong. Security is the part of IT where you get to work upstream of that, finding the gap before someone else finds it for you. I like that it rewards paranoia with actual data: a vulnerability scan, a detection rule, a hardening baseline either holds up or it doesn't. There's no ambiguity in whether the work succeeded.
What's a problem you're proud of solving? At RRMS, our exploitable vulnerability count across the Windows and Linux fleet was high enough that the backlog itself had become the risk. There was too much noise to know what actually mattered. I led a CIS & NIST-aligned hardening initiative across the environment, prioritized by actual exploitability rather than raw CVSS score, and paired it with PowerShell and Qualys API automation so remediation validation didn't depend on someone manually re-checking 5,000+ systems. That combination, better prioritization plus automated verification, took exploitable vulnerabilities down 82%. The part I'm proudest of isn't the number, it's that the process kept working after I built it, because it didn't depend on me running it by hand.
What kind of team are you looking for? A team that treats security as an engineering discipline, not a checklist, where I'm building and improving controls instead of just generating compliance reports. I do my best work with ownership over a real piece of the environment, room to automate the repetitive parts of the job, and colleagues who'll push back on my approach when they have a better one. Given my background, I'm equally comfortable being the security specialist on an infrastructure-heavy team or the infrastructure-literate voice on a security team. I'd rather solve the actual problem than stay inside a narrow lane.
